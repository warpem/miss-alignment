Abstract
Cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET) and subtomogram averaging can reveal how proteins organize and interact in their native cellular environment at molecular resolution. Accurate tilt-series alignment is essential for obtaining high-quality 3D reconstructions that preserve this rich macromolecular information. Existing algorithms for reference-free alignment that match images with predicted projections from an intermediate 3D reconstruction can fail due to incorrect assumptions about the sample thickness and shape of the features in the sample. On the other hand, algorithms that use reference-based alignments still require a sufficient initial alignment, and/or abundant, evenly-distributed, and bulky macromolecules. To solve this issue, we introduce MissAlignment - a small-scale CNN that is trained contrastively to distinguish between well-aligned and misaligned reconstructions of the sample via artificially degrading the alignment. After training, MissAlignment is used as an optimization target to correct image shifts. We show on one simulated dataset and two experimental datasets that MissAlignment outperforms field-standard methods when only considering global rigid-body alignment. For local alignment, MissAlignment is able to fit a deformation field that leads to improved template matching and high-resolution refinement. MissAlignment will set a new standard for tomogram quality, for in situ cryo-ET data.


## Introduction

### Tilt-series alignment determines tomogram interpretability

In cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET), orthogonal projection images of cryo-frozen cellular samples are captured across a tilt range of approximately ±60° with increments of 2° or 3°, forming a tilt-series. How these projections relate back to the sample volume is referred to as tilt-series alignment. Sample motion arises from two primary sources: stage shifts that occur after tilting the sample, and beam-induced motion, which manifests as non-linear deformations. A consistent alignment produces a tomogram—a 3D reconstruction of the sample—that enables both human and computational interpretation of cellular organization. Specifically, instances of macromolecules can be computationally detected and used for subtomogram averaging to obtain high-resolution reconstructions of macromolecular states within cells. However, errors in alignment during this initial phase lead to distorted biological structures in the reconstruction and severely hamper all subsequent processing steps.

The dominant alignment challenge arises from image shifts due to sample tracking errors after stage tilting. While beam-induced motion is of lesser magnitude, it remains significant for preserving high-resolution information. Traditionally, alignment methods fit these movements by assuming rigid-body motion, commonly referred to as global alignment. Different software packages vary in which parameters they optimize: IMOD offers flexible fitting of magnification, in-plane rotations, shifts, and tilt angles, whereas AreTomo assumes fixed tilt angles and optimizes only in-plane rotation and image shifts. This reflects a broader trend in the field, where properly calibrated stage tilt angles and tilt-axis angles are increasingly treated as stable parameters throughout data collection. Consequently, flexible fitting of these nominally stable parameters often inadvertently captures non-linear beam-induced motion rather than true geometric variation.

### Global tilt-series alignment remains an unsolved problem

Gold fiducial-based alignment has long dominated the field, but its application is limited to in vitro samples and is generally not applicable to in situ samples. Furthermore, fiducial-based methods are only ideal when fiducials are evenly distributed throughout the sample. Two dominant approaches have emerged for fiducial-less alignment. The first is patch-tracking, which uses cross-correlation between small cropped patches in neighboring images to create a trajectory for each tracked patch. Alignment parameters are then fitted via least-squares optimization, assuming that patch locations remain fixed in 3D space. This approach works ideally when each patch contains a single spherical feature, as such features appear identical from every angle and are therefore optimal for cross-correlation.

The second major approach, introduced in AreTomo, relies on iterative back-and-forward projection to determine shifts via cross-correlation between actual images and reprojections generated from other images in the set. This procedure aligns images sequentially from low to high tilt angles, treating the 0° image as the reference. Because this approach cannot simultaneously estimate in-plane rotation, it alternates between a shift estimation step and a rotation estimation step based on common-line alignment. Critically, shift estimation in AreTomo depends strongly on accurate sample thickness estimation to prevent spurious correlations with void areas, and on tilt-offset estimation to flatten the sample in the xy plane of volume space. The method performs best on samples with features that remain consistent along the z-dimension and have well-defined thickness.

To our knowledge, only one previously published method has approached the alignment problem using machine learning: ICE-TIDE (Debarnot et al., 2025). However, visual inspection of experimental data alignments in that work suggests suboptimal performance, and the method has not been extensively validated.

### Estimating and correcting beam-induced motion

Beam-induced motion of the sample, particularly doming effects, leads to non-linear deformations across images that cannot be captured by rigid-body models. While most fiducial-less alignment methods attempt to address beam-induced motion, they suffer from the same limitations as global alignment approaches. In certain cases, bulky macromolecules such as ribosomes can be used for high-resolution reference-based alignment (as implemented in Warp/M and RELION5). However, this approach requires an initial alignment of sufficient quality as a starting point, and depends on an even distribution of the reference structure throughout the sample—a requirement analogous to that of gold fiducial-based methods. This raises a fundamental question: how can we correct local motion in a generalizable way for diverse biological samples, rather than relying on the presence of specific abundant structures like ribosomes?

### MissAlignment: a new approach to tilt-series alignment

Here we present MissAlignment, which to our knowledge represents a unique approach to tilt-series alignment: our objective function is evaluated directly in 3D space. MissAlignment is not aware of any specific sample features and can learn to focus on what is relevant through contrastive training. The only prior information provided to the model is the generation of artificial shifts that misalign training examples, enabling the network to distinguish well-aligned from misaligned reconstructions and subsequently serve as an optimization target for alignment refinement.
