Abstract
Cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET) and subtomogram averaging can reveal how proteins organize and interact in their native cellular environment at molecular resolution. Accurate tilt-series alignment is essential for obtaining high-quality 3D reconstructions that preserve this rich macromolecular information. Existing algorithms for reference-free alignment that match images with predicted projections from an intermediate 3D reconstruction can fail due to incorrect assumptions about the sample thickness and shape of the features in the sample. On the other hand, algorithms that use reference-based alignments still require a sufficient initial alignment, and/or abundant, evenly-distributed, and bulky macromolecules. To solve this issue, we introduce MissAlignment - a small-scale CNN that is trained contrastively to distinguish between well-aligned and misaligned reconstructions of the sample via artificially degrading the alignment. After training, MissAlignment is used as an optimization target to correct image shifts. We show on one simulated dataset and two experimental datasets that MissAlignment outperforms field-standard methods when only considering global rigid-body alignment. For local alignment, MissAlignment is able to fit a deformation field that leads to improved template matching and high-resolution refinement. MissAlignment will set a new standard for tomogram quality, for in situ cryo-ET data.


## Introduction

The alignment of a tilt-series determines interpretability of tomograms
Cryo-electron tomography can provide high-resolution snap shots of macromolecules in cells if the sample can be computationally reconstructed in 3D after collection. In cryo-ET projection images of cryo-frozen cellular samples are captured across a tilt range of approximately ±60° with increments of 2° or 3°, forming a tilt-series. How these projections relate back to the sample volume is referred to as tilt-series alignment. Alignment needs to deal with sample motion that arises from two primary sources: stage shift tracking errors that occur after tilting the sample, and beam-induced motion, which manifests as non-linear deformations. A consistent alignment produces a tomogram, a 3D reconstruction of the sample, that enables both human and computational interpretation of cellular organization. Specifically, instances of macromolecules can be computationally detected and used for subtomogram averaging to obtain high-resolution reconstructions of macromolecular states within cells. However, errors in alignment during this initial phase lead to distorted biological structures in the reconstruction and hamper all subsequent processing steps.

An accurate description of sample movement in cryo-ET should deal with both global shifts and local deformation. The most significant shifts stem from sample tracking errors after stage tilting. While beam-induced motion is of lesser magnitude, it is significant for preserving high-resolution information (as illustrated in M, relion5, eman2, emClarity). Traditionally, alignment methods fit the whole group of movements by assuming a rigid-body model, commonly referred to as global alignment. Different software packages vary in which parameters they optimize, but usually include, besides translations, parameters such as magnification, in-plane rotations, and tilt angles. However, with recent quantification of beam-induced motion (refs), these extended parameters are now seen to inadvertently capture non-linear beam-induced motion rather than true geometric variation. Which is in agreement with reports/knowledge (?) that stage tilt angles and tilt-axis angles are stable parameters during data collection.

Reference-based alignments require an even distribution of the reference throughout the sample

Traditionally, tilt-series alignment has been performed using colloidal gold beads as fiducial markers due to their high contrast. When tracked accurately, these markers enable highly precise alignments that can capture local, non-rigid deformations (for example, in tomoalign). However, this approach cannot be applied to focused ion beam-milled lamellae and suffers from uneven distribution of fiducials throughout the sample, often concentrating near surfaces or void regions.

More recently, reference-based alignment methods have used abundant, rigid macromolecules within the sample as internal markers. Ribosomes are particularly well-suited for this purpose: the resolution of their subtomogram average can serve as an optimization target for refining alignment parameters. This approach allows fitting of detailed motion models necessary for high-resolution reconstructions. However, these methods share similar limitations: they require an even distribution of reference structures throughout the volume and depend on a sufficiently accurate initial alignment to converge. This raises an important question: How can local motion be corrected in a generalizable way across diverse biological samples that may lack abundant, evenly distributed macromolecular markers?

Fiducial-less global tilt-series alignment remains an unsolved problem
Two dominant approaches have emerged for fiducial-less alignment. The first is patch-tracking, which uses cross-correlation between small cropped patches in neighboring images to create a trajectory for each tracked patch. Alignment parameters are then fitted via least-squares optimization, assuming that patch locations remain fixed in 3D space. This approach works ideally when each patch contains a single spherical feature, as such features appear identical from every angle and are therefore optimal for cross-correlation.

The second major approach, introduced in Protomo and later AreTomo, relies on iterative back-and-forward projection to determine shifts via cross-correlation between actual images and reprojections generated from other images in the set. This procedure aligns images sequentially from low to high tilt angles, treating the 0° image as the reference. Because this approach cannot simultaneously estimate in-plane rotation, it alternates between a shift estimation step and a rotation estimation step based on common-line alignment. Critically, shift estimation in AreTomo depends strongly on accurate sample thickness estimation to prevent spurious correlations with void areas, and on tilt-offset estimation to flatten the sample in the xy plane of volume space. The method performs best on samples with features that remain consistent along the z-dimension and have well-defined thickness.





MissAlignment represents a unique approach to tilt-series alignment in which the objective function is evaluated directly in 3D reconstruction space. Unlike reference-based methods, MissAlignment is not designed to recognize specific sample features. Instead, it learns to focus on relevant alignment quality indicators through contrastive training, where the only prior information provided is the synthetic generation of shifts that degrade alignment. This feature-agnostic design enables generalization across diverse biological samples without requiring abundant fiducial markers or specific macromolecular references.
